John Maynard Keynes

Introduction

John Maynard Keynes was one of the most influential economists, perhaps of all time, and has a branch of economics ("Keyensian Economics") named after him. His ideas formed the basis of macroeconomics; hence, he is known as the "father of macroeconomics". He is best known for his work regarding governmental intervention in markets and mixed economies, and has a book titled "The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money", which was published in 1936.

Timeline

  • 1883 - Keynes is born in Cambridge.

  • 1897 - Keynes gets a scholarship to Eton College.

  • 1902 - Keynes studies in King's College, Cambridge.

  • 1906 - Keynes starts his career in the public sector working as a clerk in the India Office.

  • 1914 - World War 1 begins.

  • 1915 - Keynes joins the UK Treasury and acts as an advisor to the government.

  • 1918 - World War 1 ends.

  • 1919 - Keynes turns down an offer to become chairman of the British Bank of Northern Commerce.

  • 1922 - Keynes advocates for the reduction of German reparations.

  • 1925 - Keynes gets married to Lydia Lopokova.

  • 1929 - The Great Depression results in massive financial losses and low confidence in the economy.

  • 1936 - Keynes publishes his book "The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money".

  • 1939 - World War 2 begins.

  • 1945 - World War 2 ends.

  • 1946 - Keynes dies aged 62.

Keynesian Economics

"An economic policy advocating a mixed economy, with some state ownership of industries and economic invention to maintain high employment and ensure social cohesion."

Keynesian economics argue that demand drives supply, and that during a recession, governments should increase spending to create jobs and increase demand, even if it means going into debt.

They also believe that aggregate supply is the same in the short and long term, so they do not have a short run aggregate supply curve, instead opting for a long run aggregate supply curve that is near perfectly elastic at low levels of real output, and near perfectly inelastic at high levels of real output.
